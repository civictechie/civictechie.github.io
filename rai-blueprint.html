WHY THIS FORMAT?

Built on Familiar Foundations

THE FRAMEWORK

Blueprint Structure

5 Swim Lanes + Responsibility Layer

What patients do throughout their journey

Provider Actions

Frontstage (Visible)

Backstage (Invisible)

AI algorithms, data processing, APIs

Support Systems

Infrastructure, third parties, data

EXAMPLE USE CASE

AI-Powered Sepsis Early Warning System

Five critical stages where responsibility gaps emerge

01
Patient Enrollment & Setup

Sensors placed, monitoring activated, baseline data collected. Gap: Who ensures patients understand data usage for research?

02
Continuous Monitoring

AI recalculates risk every 5 minutes. Conflict: Algorithm optimized for sensitivity vs. nurses experiencing alert fatigue.

03
High-Risk Alert üö®

Critical handoff point. Gap: Alert sent at 3:15am during understaffed shift‚Äîwho's accountable for the 90-minute delay?

04
Clinical Response

Physician overrides AI recommendation. Conflict: If patient worsens, who's accountable‚Äîphysician or AI?

05
Post-Discharge Data Use

Data anonymized and sold to research consortium. Massive gap: Patient never knew data would be monetized.

WORKSHOP FORMAT

60-Minute Blueprint Session

15 MIN
Preparation
  • Print 6-layer swim lane structure
  • Gather colored sticky notes
  • Blue = actions/touchpoints
  • Yellow = systems/infrastructure
  • Red = gaps/conflicts
  • Green = knowledge needs
30 MIN
Mapping
  • Map patient & provider journey
  • Add frontstage interfaces
  • Map backstage AI processes
  • Add support systems
  • Draw handoff connections
15 MIN
Analysis
  • Ask: "Who's accountable if this fails?"
  • Mark gaps, conflicts, knowledge needs
  • Identify 3 highest-risk areas
  • Plan design interventions
WHY IT WORKS

Key Advantages

vs. Traditional Responsibility Matrix
  • Shows responsibility over time (journey stages)
  • Makes handoffs visible (highest-risk zones)
  • Separates visible from invisible (where knowledge gaps hide)
  • Familiar format for designers (easy adoption)
vs. Stakeholder Map
  • Shows when each actor is involved, not just that they exist
  • Reveals sequential dependencies in responsibilities
  • Makes backstage/support systems explicit
  • Identifies critical handoff points
BACKGROUND

About This Framework

Why We Built This

AI systems in healthcare are incredibly complex ecosystems involving patients, providers, algorithms, devices, cloud infrastructure, EHR systems, insurance companies, and research partners. When something goes wrong, responsibility often falls through the cracks‚Äîeveryone blames someone else, and patients suffer.

Traditional responsibility matrices and stakeholder maps don't capture the temporal, sequential nature of AI-powered healthcare journeys. We needed a tool that could:

  • ‚Üí Show responsibility over time, not just static organizational charts
  • ‚Üí Make visible what happens "backstage" in AI systems
  • ‚Üí Identify handoff points where responsibility gaps emerge
  • ‚Üí Use a format already familiar to UX designers

Research Foundation

This blueprint adapts service design methodology for AI responsibility mapping. It's based on research published in AI and Ethics on embedding responsibility in intelligent systems, which argues that responsibility must be designed into the ecosystem from the start‚Äînot retrofitted after incidents occur.

The framework has been tested with healthcare UX teams, clinical informatics departments, and AI product teams across hospital systems, medical device companies, and health tech startups.
